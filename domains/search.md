# Search Costs: How AI Transforms the Economics of Finding

Search costs are the costs of finding information, people, resources, and opportunities. In Transaction Cost Economics, they represent what you must spend *before* a transaction can even begin: identifying what exists, who has it, what it's worth, and whether it fits your needs. Search costs have historically been a major structural barrier to economic efficiency --- entire industries (recruiting, consulting, brokerage, advertising) exist primarily because search is expensive.

AI transforms search costs in ways that are structurally deep and, in several cases, near-certain. The following premises map the most important of these transformations.

---

## Premise 1: The Asymmetric Collapse --- AI Destroys Search Costs for Information but Not for Judgment

### Claim
AI radically reduces the cost of finding and synthesizing *information*, but the cost of determining what that information *means for a specific decision* remains high and increasingly becomes the binding constraint.

### Reasoning
Search costs have always had two components: (1) finding relevant information, and (2) evaluating its fitness for purpose. AI obliterates the first. An LLM can synthesize the contents of thousands of documents in seconds --- something that once required teams of analysts, lawyers, or consultants spending weeks. But the second component --- determining whether that information is reliable, relevant to *your* particular context, and worth acting on --- requires judgment that is irreducibly contextual.

This is not a temporary limitation waiting for better models. It is structural. Judgment requires understanding the *specific situation* of the decision-maker: their constraints, risk tolerance, strategic position, organizational politics, regulatory environment. This context is precisely what AI lacks access to, not because of technical limits, but because it is private information held by the decision-maker. Even a perfect information retrieval system cannot tell you whether to act on what it finds, because action depends on context it cannot observe.

The result is an asymmetry: as information search approaches zero cost, judgment becomes the scarce factor. The ratio of "finding" to "deciding" inverts. Where organizations once spent 80% of their time finding information and 20% evaluating it, AI flips this toward 20/80 --- or more extreme.

### Evidence
- **Legal services:** AI tools like Harvey and CoCounsel perform legal research in seconds that took junior associates hours. But law firms report that the bottleneck has shifted to senior partners interpreting findings in client-specific context. Joshua Yuvaraj's research on the "verification-value paradox" formalizes this: *Net value of AI = efficiency gains - verification costs*. In serious legal work, verification often consumes most of the time saved.
- **Medical diagnosis:** AI matches or exceeds radiologists in image analysis accuracy, yet the value of physicians increasingly concentrates on interpreting findings within a patient's full clinical context --- comorbidities, history, patient preferences --- things the scan cannot capture.
- **Consulting:** Tools like AlphaSense and Palantir rapidly generate market research and competitive analysis. Yet consulting firms maintain high fees because clients pay not for the research but for the *interpretation* of that research in their specific strategic context. LogicSource reports that AI reduces the time for research and modeling in consulting but firms keep fees stable because "consultants can service more clients concurrently without a proportional drop in pricing."
- **Procurement:** BCG reports AI can execute spend analysis "90% faster than manual data analysis" --- but the strategic decisions about *what to do* with that analysis remain human-dependent.

### Implications
1. **Professional services bifurcate.** The "finding" layer of professional work (legal research, financial analysis, market research, medical imaging) becomes commodity. The "judgment" layer (strategic advice, contextual interpretation, complex decision-making) becomes the locus of value. Firms that still bundle these will be undercut by AI on the commodity layer and outperformed by specialists on the judgment layer.
2. **Pricing models must change.** Hourly billing becomes indefensible when AI does the research in seconds. The shift to outcome-based, fixed-fee, or value-based pricing accelerates. LogicSource already documents clients negotiating 20% reductions on legal work by benchmarking AI-enabled efficiencies.
3. **"Judgment infrastructure" becomes an investable category.** Tools that help people make better *decisions* with abundant information (not tools that help them *find* information) are structurally undervalued. Decision-support, scenario modeling, and contextual reasoning tools sit in this gap.

### Certainty Rating
**High.** The asymmetry between information retrieval and judgment is not a bet on AI capability --- it is a consequence of the structural difference between public information (which AI can process) and private context (which it cannot observe). This holds regardless of how good models get, because the bottleneck is epistemic, not computational.

### Interactions
- **Search x Production:** When search for information is cheap, the constraint on production shifts from "finding out how" to "deciding what to make and for whom" --- judgment again.
- **Search x Enforcement:** Cheap information search means more data is *available* for enforcement, but verifying its accuracy becomes a new enforcement cost (see Premise 4).
- **Search x Bargaining:** When both sides of a negotiation have access to the same AI-surfaced information, information asymmetry decreases but *interpretive asymmetry* (who understands the information better in context) becomes the new source of bargaining power.

## Premise 2: Expert Knowledge Becomes Commodity; Expertise Does Not

### Claim
AI commoditizes the *knowledge content* that experts possess, but the ability to deploy knowledge in novel, ambiguous, or high-stakes situations --- what we might call expertise-in-action --- remains scarce and becomes relatively more valuable.

### Reasoning
Historically, professionals in law, medicine, finance, and consulting derived much of their value from possessing knowledge that was expensive to acquire and hard to find. A specialist physician's value came partly from knowing rare diagnostic patterns; a financial advisor's value came partly from understanding investment strategies; a lawyer's value came partly from knowing case law. AI collapses the search cost for all of this knowledge to near zero.

But there is a crucial distinction between *knowing the answer to a well-posed question* and *knowing which question to ask in an ambiguous situation*. The first is what AI commoditizes. The second --- the ability to frame problems, recognize which patterns apply in novel situations, make judgment calls under uncertainty, and take responsibility for outcomes --- is what constitutes expertise-in-action. This is not merely "soft skills" or "emotional intelligence" dressed up in new language. It is a genuinely different cognitive capacity: the ability to operate in domains where the relevant variables are not yet identified, where the problem itself is ill-defined, and where the cost of error is high.

The structural argument is: as the floor of accessible knowledge rises (everyone now has "expert-level" access via AI), the differentiator shifts upward to the capacity that *cannot* be accessed via query --- the capacity to operate in ambiguity.

### Evidence
- **Box CEO Aaron Levie** (January 2026): "In a world where everyone has access to the same expert intelligence, how does a company differentiate? Context." He argues the force-multiplier is proprietary information and institutional knowledge --- things that are specific to a firm and not in the training data.
- **MIT Sloan Review** (October 2025): "When knowledge becomes commoditized, its value paradoxically shifts from the content to the *context*." The article documents three transformations in how expertise creates value post-AI.
- **HBR's "Strategy in an Era of Abundant Expertise"** (2025): Documents the cost of using top-tier AI dropping by over 99% in two years. The implication is that any knowledge accessible through training data is approaching commodity.
- **JPMorgan's COIN system** reviewed 360,000 hours of lawyer work on commercial loan agreements. But the real transformation was not eliminating lawyers --- it was redirecting them from document review to "complex deal structuring and relationship management." The commoditized layer was automated; the judgment layer was elevated.
- **Healthcare:** AI diagnostic tools bring specialist-level pattern recognition to underserved areas, acting as "virtual experts." But physicians remain essential for treatment decisions involving patient preferences, comorbidities, and ethical trade-offs --- the ambiguous, high-stakes layer.

### Implications
1. **The lawyer-cleaner inversion applies broadly.** The argument Ziqi has made before: AI compresses the skill premium for *knowledge-heavy* work more than for *context-heavy* or *physical* work. A lawyer's research skills are more automatable than a cleaner's spatial navigation. The scarcity gradient of human labor shifts toward roles that require presence, context, or ambiguity-navigation.
2. **Education must pivot from knowledge transfer to judgment development.** If AI gives everyone access to what an expert knows, education's value must come from teaching people how to *use* knowledge in ill-defined situations. This is a structural shift, not a pedagogical preference.
3. **"Expertise arbitrage" becomes a viable business model.** The gap between what AI can surface (information) and what experienced professionals can do with it (deploy in context) creates an arbitrage opportunity: firms that pair AI information retrieval with deep domain expertise can serve more clients at lower cost while preserving the judgment premium.

### Certainty Rating
**High.** The commoditization of codified knowledge is already visibly happening. The distinction between knowledge-as-content and expertise-as-action is well-established in philosophy of knowledge (tacit vs. explicit knowledge, Polanyi) and is empirically confirmed by the pattern across every profession where AI has been deployed: the routine layer gets automated, the judgment layer gets elevated.

### Interactions
- **Search x Production:** Commoditized expert knowledge lowers the *production* cost of knowledge work --- drafting contracts, generating financial models, writing code. But the *design* of what to produce remains expertise-dependent.
- **Search x Coordination:** When everyone has access to the same expert knowledge, coordination becomes harder because there are more "informed" participants with potentially conflicting interpretations. The coordination cost of disagreement among knowledgeable parties may increase.

## Premise 3: Reduced Search Costs Create a Verification Burden That Partially Offsets the Gains

### Claim
As AI makes it nearly costless to generate and surface information, the cost of *verifying* that information becomes a significant new friction --- partially offsetting the search cost savings and creating demand for new trust infrastructure.

### Reasoning
This is perhaps the most counterintuitive premise, because it suggests that collapsing search costs does not deliver the full savings one might naively expect. The mechanism is straightforward:

1. AI makes it cheap to *produce* information (text, analysis, content, job postings, applications).
2. AI makes it cheap to *find* information (search, summarize, synthesize).
3. Therefore, the volume of information explodes.
4. But the reliability of that information is uneven --- AI hallucinates, generates plausible-but-wrong outputs, and can produce convincing-looking content that is substantively empty.
5. Therefore, the cost of *verifying* whether any particular piece of information is accurate, relevant, and trustworthy increases --- or at minimum, does not decrease proportionally.

This is not an AI failure that will be "fixed" with better models. It is a structural consequence of making production and search cheap: when anyone can generate expert-sounding content at zero marginal cost, the signal-to-noise ratio degrades, and verification becomes the new bottleneck.

The economic parallel is clear: when the cost of producing currency drops to zero, you get inflation and the need for anti-counterfeiting measures. When the cost of producing information drops to zero, you get information inflation and the need for verification infrastructure.

### Evidence
- **Legal profession:** Damien Charlotin's database documents over 300 instances of AI hallucinations in court filings, up from a handful in 2023. The verification-value paradox is becoming formalized: "if verification of AI content by an expert takes as much time as creating it from scratch, the economic benefit of the technology disappears."
- **Labor markets:** A landmark field experiment by Wiles & Horton (MIT/BU, February 2026) on a major online labor platform found that AI-assisted job posting creation increased postings by 19% but *did not increase hires*. The mechanism: AI made it easy to post low-intent, generic job descriptions that wasted jobseeker time. "The per job post loss to jobseeker welfare is six times larger than the increase to employer welfare." The search cost savings on the employer side were more than undone by verification costs imposed on the jobseeker side.
- **Content ecosystems:** Forbes (February 2026) documents "information overload 2.0" --- AI-generated summaries of summaries creating layers of abstraction where no one retains meaningful understanding. "Teams may only have half-digested insights, misaligned assumptions, or entirely different interpretations of the same AI-generated content."
- **Code review:** Anthropic launched a dedicated AI code review tool (March 2026) specifically to address the flood of AI-generated code, at $15-25 per review --- verification as a product category emerging in response to cheap AI production.
- **Content authenticity:** Pangram Labs (founded by ex-Google/Tesla researchers) builds AI detection tools. The Content Credentials initiative is developing "human-made" labeling standards. Diplo's analysis: "Trust broke before AI capability matured" --- the erosion of trust began with *abundant* synthetic content, not *perfect* synthetic content.

### Implications
1. **Verification becomes an investable infrastructure layer.** Just as the internet created demand for payment infrastructure (PayPal) and identity infrastructure (SSL certificates), cheap AI-generated information creates demand for verification infrastructure: provenance tracking, authenticity certification, quality assurance tools.
2. **"AI-free" or "human-verified" becomes a premium signal.** Analogous to "organic" food labeling --- not because AI content is necessarily worse, but because verification at scale is impractical, so labels become decision heuristics. Diplo's analysis explicitly predicts "human authorship becomes a premium."
3. **Two-sided markets face a design problem.** The Wiles & Horton finding is generalizable: any platform where AI reduces the cost of posting/listing on one side may degrade matching efficiency by flooding the other side with low-quality signals. Platforms must reintroduce "screening devices" that are lighter than the old friction but sufficient to maintain signal quality.
4. **The net savings from AI search cost reduction are real but smaller than headline numbers suggest.** The naive calculation (AI does in seconds what took hours, therefore saves hours) ignores the new verification costs that emerge. Actual net savings require subtracting verification burden.

### Certainty Rating
**Medium-High.** The direction of this effect is near-certain --- cheap production always increases verification burden. The magnitude is uncertain: better AI models will reduce hallucination rates, but the volume of AI-generated content will increase faster, and adversarial uses (deliberate misinformation) amplify the problem. The net effect is likely to be a persistent verification tax on AI-generated information, though it may decrease as a percentage over time.

### Interactions
- **Search x Enforcement:** This is the deepest interaction. Verification *is* a form of enforcement. As search costs drop and information volume explodes, enforcement costs (ensuring accuracy, compliance, quality) rise. These two categories are in structural tension.
- **Search x Bargaining:** When both parties in a negotiation can generate plausible-sounding analysis, the ability to *verify* claims becomes a source of bargaining power. Information asymmetry may be replaced by "verification asymmetry."
- **Search x Coordination:** Verification burden makes coordination harder --- if team members are working from AI-generated summaries that are subtly different or unreliable, alignment costs increase even as information access costs decrease.

## Premise 4: Search Cost Collapse Restructures Matching Markets Toward Congestion, Not Efficiency

### Claim
In two-sided matching markets (hiring, dating, B2B procurement, freelancing), AI-driven search cost reductions tend to increase *volume* on both sides without proportionally increasing *match quality*, creating congestion that shifts the bottleneck from finding candidates to filtering them.

### Reasoning
Standard economic theory predicts that lower search costs should improve matching efficiency: if it's cheaper to find potential partners, you should find better matches. But this model assumes that the number of searchers and the quality of signals remain constant. AI violates both assumptions simultaneously.

When AI reduces the cost of searching, it also reduces the cost of *being searched for* (generating applications, listings, profiles). This means more participants enter the market on both sides, and each participant generates more attempts. The result is not a more efficient market but a more *congested* one. The analogy is highway expansion: adding lanes (reducing the cost of driving) induces more driving, and congestion often returns to previous levels.

The deeper structural issue is that matching markets rely on *costly signals* --- effort expended in crafting an application, writing a job description, or personalizing a message --- to convey intent and quality. When AI reduces these costs, it also degrades the signals. A hand-written cover letter used to signal genuine interest; an AI-generated one signals nothing except access to ChatGPT.

### Evidence
- **The Wiles & Horton experiment (2026):** This is the strongest causal evidence. AI-assisted job posting *increased postings by 19%* but *reduced hiring rate proportionally*. Treated employers' posts were "more semantically similar to each other" --- more generic --- reducing their information content. Jobseekers applied to more jobs that didn't lead to hires. "The dominant driver of reduced hires comes from marginal postings with low hiring intent."
- **HBR (January 2026):** "AI Has Made Hiring Worse --- But It Can Still Help." Documents the paradox of AI in recruitment: more applications, faster screening, but worse outcomes because signal quality has degraded on both sides.
- **Recruiting industry data:** AI recruitment tools reduce cost-per-hire by ~30% (SHRM), but hiring managers report drowning in AI-generated applications that are harder to distinguish from each other. The search cost savings on sourcing are being consumed by filtering costs.
- **Earlier precedent:** Kroft & Pope (2014), Kuhn & Mansour (2014), Kuhn & Skuterud (2004) all found that the internet "dramatically lowered search and dissemination costs without clear evidence of increased match formation in labor markets." AI is repeating this pattern at greater intensity.

### Implications
1. **Filtering becomes more valuable than finding.** The scarce capability in matching markets shifts from *sourcing* (finding candidates/opportunities) to *screening* (distinguishing genuine high-quality matches from noise). This is the inverse of the traditional recruiting value proposition.
2. **Costly signals will be reintroduced deliberately.** Markets will evolve new signaling mechanisms that are expensive enough to separate serious participants from casual ones, but less wasteful than the old frictions. Examples: paid applications, skill assessments, video introductions, stake-based signals (deposits, commitments). The Wiles & Horton paper explicitly recommends "lightweight screening devices."
3. **Platform design becomes a critical differentiator.** Platforms that naively reduce friction on both sides will degrade. Platforms that *intelligently manage* friction --- reducing it where it's wasteful, preserving it where it signals quality --- will win. This is a non-obvious design principle: the best matching platform is not the one with the least friction.
4. **"Matching intelligence" is an investable category.** Not search technology (which is commodity) but matching *quality* technology --- systems that maintain signal integrity in high-volume, AI-flooded markets.

### Certainty Rating
**Medium-High.** The congestion dynamic is already empirically documented and theoretically well-grounded (it mirrors induced demand in transportation economics and is consistent with Akerlof's market-for-lemons logic applied to signal degradation). The specific form this takes in each market will vary, but the structural tendency toward congestion when both-sided costs drop simultaneously is robust.

### Interactions
- **Search x Bargaining:** Congestion in matching markets means that once a match is found, the *bargaining* dynamics change --- if both sides know the market is noisy, the party that has successfully verified a good match has leverage, because the other side knows re-entering the noisy market is costly.
- **Search x Coordination:** Congested markets require more sophisticated coordination mechanisms (algorithms, market design, intermediaries) to function. The demand for coordination infrastructure rises as search costs fall.
- **Search x Production:** When it's easy to find producers/suppliers via AI but hard to verify quality, the production side faces pressure to develop stronger quality signals --- certifications, track records, portfolios --- that can cut through the noise.

## Premise 5: The Locus of Search Shifts From "What Exists" to "What Is Right For Me"

### Claim
AI transforms the fundamental nature of search from a problem of *discovery* (finding what exists) to a problem of *fit* (determining what matches my specific needs, preferences, and context) --- and this second problem requires different infrastructure than the first.

### Reasoning
Pre-AI search was primarily a discovery problem. You didn't know what was out there. Google's core value was revealing the existence of relevant web pages. A recruiter's core value was knowing which candidates existed. A consultant's core value was knowing what the market data said.

AI dissolves the discovery problem. With sufficiently capable AI, the question "what exists?" is cheaply answerable for most domains. What remains expensive --- and becomes the new core of search --- is the question "of everything that exists, what is *right for me*?" This is a categorically different problem because:

1. It requires modeling the *searcher*, not just the *searched*. Discovery requires understanding what's in the world; fit requires understanding the specific person or organization doing the searching.
2. It is inherently personalized and cannot be answered generically. Two people with the same query may need radically different results based on their context.
3. It is dynamic --- what's "right for me" changes as my situation changes, as I learn, as my goals evolve.

This shift explains why traditional keyword search (and even AI-enhanced keyword search) is structurally insufficient for the emerging search problem. The next generation of search infrastructure must be *recommender systems that deeply model the searcher*, not just search engines that deeply index the searched.

### Evidence
- **AI career agents:** Emerge EdTech describes the frontier of hiring as "AI career agents" that "understand your experiences, goals, values, and career aspirations" and "proactively identify roles that truly fit." This is fit-based, not discovery-based search.
- **Healthcare:** AI diagnostic tools are moving from "what disease matches these symptoms?" (discovery) to "what treatment plan fits this patient's full context?" (fit). The shift is from pattern-matching on the disease side to modeling on the patient side.
- **Financial services:** Robo-advisors have evolved from simple portfolio allocation (discovery of optimal assets) toward holistic financial planning that models individual goals, risk tolerance, life stage, and tax situation (fit to the individual). The AI Index Report 2025 notes that 49% of businesses adopting AI in service operations report cost savings, driven largely by personalization.
- **Procurement:** McKinsey's 2025 procurement study emphasizes that AI's value is moving beyond finding cheaper suppliers (discovery) toward identifying suppliers that fit specific strategic, sustainability, and risk criteria (fit). Centers of excellence now focus on "cost engineering" and "should-cost modeling" --- fit-based analysis, not supplier discovery.
- **Content recommendation:** The entire shift from search-based media consumption (Google, manual browsing) to recommendation-based consumption (TikTok, Spotify Discover, Netflix) is precisely this transition from discovery to fit.

### Implications
1. **"Who is the searcher?" becomes as important as "what is being searched for."** Products that model the user deeply --- understanding their context, preferences, constraints, and goals --- will outperform products that merely index the world more thoroughly. This is a paradigm shift in search infrastructure.
2. **Privacy and data become strategic inputs to search quality.** If fit-based search requires deep knowledge of the searcher, then the data the searcher is willing to share becomes a key input. This creates a tension: better search requires more personal data, but privacy concerns limit data sharing. Products that navigate this tension (achieving high fit with minimal data, or offering compelling enough value that users share willingly) will win.
3. **The incumbent advantage shifts.** In discovery-based search, the advantage goes to whoever indexes the most (Google). In fit-based search, the advantage goes to whoever understands the user best. This may favor platforms with deep user interaction data (workplace tools, financial platforms, health apps) over general-purpose search engines.
4. **Advisory businesses transform into "fit engines."** Financial advisors, career coaches, executive recruiters, personal shoppers --- anyone whose value was partly "I know what's out there" and partly "I know what's right for you" --- will see the first component commoditized. Those who double down on the second component, augmented by AI, can serve more people better. Those who don't will be disintermediated.

### Certainty Rating
**Medium-High.** The shift from discovery to fit is already well underway (recommendation systems are the dominant paradigm in media consumption). The extension of this pattern to professional services, hiring, procurement, and other high-stakes domains is structurally likely but will take longer and face more resistance because the stakes of "poor fit" are higher than in content consumption. The direction is clear; the timeline is uncertain.

### Interactions
- **Search x Production:** When search becomes fit-based, production can become more targeted. If you know exactly *who* wants *what*, you can produce to demand rather than producing for a market and hoping to be found. This tightens the coupling between search and production.
- **Search x Bargaining:** Fit-based search changes bargaining dynamics because the searcher who has found a high-fit match values it more (they know how rare good fit is in a noisy market) and may be willing to pay a premium. This reduces price sensitivity for high-fit matches.
- **Search x Coordination:** Fit-based search enables more precise coordination --- if you can accurately match people to roles, tasks to skills, and resources to needs, coordination overhead drops. This is the positive-sum version of the premise: when search gets the *fit* right (not just the *finding* right), coordination costs decrease.

## Summary of Interactions Across the Five Categories

The search cost premises interact with the other four transaction cost categories in patterns that are worth flagging for the other agents:

| Interaction | Key Dynamic |
|---|---|
| **Search x Bargaining** | Information asymmetry decreases, but *interpretive asymmetry* and *verification asymmetry* replace it as sources of bargaining power |
| **Search x Enforcement** | More information is available for enforcement, but the verification burden (ensuring that information is accurate) becomes a new enforcement cost --- these categories are in structural tension |
| **Search x Production** | Cheap search enables more targeted production (produce for known demand, not speculative markets), but the design/judgment layer of production remains expensive |
| **Search x Coordination** | Congested markets increase coordination costs; fit-based search decreases them. Net effect depends on whether market design keeps pace with volume increases |
